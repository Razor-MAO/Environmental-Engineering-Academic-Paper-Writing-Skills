# Writing Style Guide: Environmental Engineering / Water Research

> This profile synthesizes writing patterns from 12 published papers by multiple research groups in environmental engineering and water/wastewater research (Water Research, Environmental Science & Technology, Chemical Engineering Journal, Journal of Hazardous Materials, Environment International, ACS Environmental Au). The analyzed authors include the groups of Tao Liu (PolyU / UQ), Yiwen Liu (Tianjin University), Senlin Shao (Wuhan University), and Yanying He (Hunan Agricultural University).

---

## 1. Vocabulary

### 1.1 High-Frequency Technical Terms and Fixed Collocations

The following terms appear consistently across all 12 papers (most frequent first):

**Core field terms:** wastewater treatment, nitrogen removal, removal efficiency, nutrient removal, greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, microbial community, membrane distillation (MD), biofilm, hydraulic retention time (HRT), solids retention time (SRT), dissolved oxygen (DO), chemical oxygen demand (COD), total nitrogen (TN), dissolved organic matter (DOM)

**Process-specific terms:** partial nitritation, anammox, denitrification, nitrification, nitrifier denitrification, heterotrophic denitrification, autotrophic nitrogen removal, adsorption, passivation, crystallization, scaling, fouling, wetting

**Methodology terms:** batch tests, long-term operation, continuous-flow, 16S rRNA gene sequencing, mass balance, mathematical modeling, in situ observation, optical coherence tomography (OCT), inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS)

**Evaluation terms:** removal efficiency, relative abundance, contribution, accumulation, conversion, performance, robustness, stability, feasibility

### 1.2 Verb Preferences

- **Strong preference for passive constructions across all sections**, especially Methods and Results:
  - "was developed / proposed / investigated / conducted / achieved"
  - "was observed / demonstrated / revealed / found / detected"
  - "have been reported / shown / demonstrated / studied / investigated"
  - "were collected / measured / analyzed / calculated / determined"

- **Action verbs used primarily in Discussion/Conclusions (with "This study/Our results"):**
  - "This study demonstrates / reveals / shows / indicates / suggests"
  - "Our results highlight / underscore / confirm / imply / provide"
  - "These findings suggest / indicate / support / point to / advance"

- **When "We" is used (rare, <5 instances per paper):**
  - Almost exclusively in "In this study, we developed/proposed/investigated..."
  - Beyond that, third-person passive is strongly preferred

### 1.3 Hedging Usage

- **Moderate hedging frequency:** 2.8–7.2 occurrences per 1,000 words
- **Typical hedging verbs:** "may", "could", "suggest", "indicate", "might"
- **Hedging pattern:**
  - Introduction: moderate hedging ("may be attributed to", "is expected to", "has been proposed as")
  - Results: minimal hedging (direct statements of what was observed)
  - Discussion: heavy hedging returns ("might be explained by", "could be associated with", "may reflect")
  - Conclusions: almost no hedging (confident statements of findings)

- **Dispreferred hedging forms** (rarely appear):
  - "it is possible that" (replaced with "may/could + verb")
  - "it seems that" (too vague)
  - "perhaps" (too informal)
  - "it is worth noting that" (briefly used only in Discussion transitions)

### 1.4 Transition Word Preferences

**Must use (high frequency in all papers):**
- "However" – most frequent transition, used to introduce gap or counterpoint
- "Therefore" – causal conclusion
- "Notably" – used at sentence beginning to highlight key findings (very frequent)
- "Additionally" / "Furthermore" – adding evidence
- "In contrast" – comparison
- "Nevertheless" – concession before counter-argument
- "Meanwhile" – simultaneous events
- "Collectively" – summarizing multiple findings (mainly Discussion)

**Must avoid:**
- "Hence" – used only 0-1 times per paper, sounds dated
- "Thus" – less preferred than "Therefore"
- "Henceforth" / "Thereby" – almost never used
- "Firstly / Secondly / Lastly" – replaced by numbered lists (1, 2, 3)

## 2. Sentence Structure

### 2.1 Sentence Length

- **Average:** 17–24 words per sentence (field standard)
- **Pattern by section:**
  - Introduction: 20–30 words (complex, citation-packed sentences)
  - Methods: 10–18 words (short, imperative-passive)
  - Results: 15–22 words (moderate, direct)
  - Discussion: 18–25 words (analytical, some complex structures)

### 2.2 Passive vs. Active Voice

| Section | Passive | Active | "We" usage |
|---------|---------|--------|------------|
| Introduction | Heavy: "has been reported/shown" | Moderate: for citations "X reported" | Rare |
| Methods | ~95%: "was collected/measured" | None | None |
| Results | ~80%: "was observed/demonstrated" | ~20%: "The results show" | Very rare |
| Discussion | ~60%: "may be attributed to" | ~40%: "This finding suggests" | "In this study, we" |
| Conclusions | ~30% | ~70%: "This study demonstrates" | "We demonstrated" |

**Rule:** Methods must use passive exclusively. Results should be passive-dominant with occasional active for key findings. Discussion should mix active (for own findings) and passive (for general knowledge). Conclusions prefer active voice.

### 2.3 Common Sentence Structures

**Background/Context (Introduction):**
- "X has been widely recognized / reported / applied as..."
- "X has attracted increasing attention over the past decades as..."
- "X is considered a promising approach for..."
- "Despite these advances, two major challenges remain..."
- "To date, X has been investigated for..."

**Gap Statement (Introduction):**
- "However, [gap] is still poorly understood / remains unclear / has not been well investigated"
- "Nevertheless, the underlying mechanism of X remains elusive"
- "A critical technical challenge is the mismatch between..."

**Own Study Introduction:**
- "In this study, we developed / proposed / investigated / designed..."
- "Herein, we report / demonstrate / show..."
- "The primary objective(s) of this study include(s):"

**Observing Results:**
- "The results showed / demonstrated / indicated that..."
- "It was observed / found / noted that..."
- "Notably, X was significantly higher / lower / different..."

**Drawing Inferences (Discussion):**
- "This finding suggests / indicates / implies that..."
- "This could be attributed to / explained by / associated with..."
- "X may contribute to / result from / reflect..."

**Conclusions:**
- "This study demonstrated / revealed / confirmed that..."
- "The main conclusions include: ... followed by numbered list"
- "These findings collectively suggest / provide / highlight..."

### 2.4 Sentence Opening Diversity

- Most common openings: "The", "In", "This", "However,", "These", "For", "Notably"
- **Mechanical variation rule:** Avoid more than 2 consecutive sentences starting with the same word
- "However" is frequently placed at **sentence beginning** (not embedded mid-sentence)
- "Notably" nearly always appears at **sentence beginning** to mark an important finding
- "In this study" is used exclusively at transition from Introduction to the study's own work

## 3. Paragraph and Logic Structure

### 3.1 Topic Sentence Position

- **Always at the beginning** of each paragraph across all sections
- The first sentence of each paragraph in Introduction establishes the single claim/context for that paragraph
- In Results, the topic sentence states the main observational finding
- In Discussion, the topic sentence states the interpretive claim

### 3.2 Paragraph Development Logic

**Introduction paragraphs follow:**
`
Claim/Context sentence → Supporting evidence with citations (2-5 citations) → Gap/limitation → Transition
`

**Results paragraphs follow:**
`
Key finding statement → Supporting data (values, statistics) → Brief interpretation → Transition
`

**Discussion paragraphs follow:**
`
Interpretive claim → Evidence from this study → Literature comparison → Implication / Future direction
`

**Introduction standard move sequence (paper-level):**
1. Establish the field/technology importance
2. Review key advances and current state
3. Identify specific unsolved problem/gap
4. State how this study addresses the gap
5. List specific objectives

### 3.3 Paragraph Closure

- **Never** a stand-alone summary within body paragraphs
- Paragraphs typically end with:
  - A transitional sentence pointing to the next idea
  - An implication or significance statement
  - A contrast or remaining challenge
- "Therefore" and "Thus" used for summarizing transitions
- "However" and "Nevertheless" for counterpoint transitions

### 3.4 Conclusion Structure

- Opener: "This study demonstrated/revealed that..."
- 4-8 numbered bullet points with specific findings and values
- Final sentence: Broader significance / implication statement
- Numbered lists use (1), (2), (3) format with periods after each item
- Each conclusion point is a complete sentence with specific data

## 4. Tone and Register

### 4.1 Formality Level

- **High but not archaic:** Formal academic register without unnecessary flourish
- **No figurative language, idioms, or metaphors**
- **No rhetorical questions** in body text
- **Contractions are strictly forbidden** (don't, doesn't, can't → do not, does not, cannot)
- **Quotations in " "** used only for species names (Candidatus "Methylomirabilis oxyfera")

### 4.2 Conclusion Confidence

- **High confidence in own experimental findings** (direct statements, minimal hedging)
- **Conservative when extrapolating** ("These findings suggest that X could be applied to..." not "X will revolutionize...")
- **Acknowledges limitations explicitly** ("Further investigation is needed to disclose...")
- **Future outlook:** uses "may", "could", "is expected to" not "will"
- **Never uses "novel" or "first"** in abstract unless genuinely first demonstration (verified in text)

### 4.3 Fixed Expressions (group-specific habits)

- "has been attracting increasing attention" or "has attracted increasing attention"
- "It was hypothesized that..." (only in Discussion introductions)
- "This is the first study to demonstrate..." (rare, significant claims only)
- "It should be noted that..." (used for important caveats in Discussion)
- "Consistent with previous studies..." (in Discussion comparison)
- "In agreement with [Author], the present study found..."
- "The results presented herein show..."
- "Taken together, these results suggest..."

### 4.4 Dispreferred Patterns to Avoid

- **Overuse of "importantly":** Over half the papers use it rarely; not a staple transition
- **"In conclusion":** Replaced by section heading "5. Conclusions" — no need for in-text label
- **"As mentioned above":** Rare, replaced by logical flow
- **"As we know":** Too informal, never used
- **Sequential signposting** "First, ... Second, ... Finally, ...": Used only within enumerated lists
- **"respectively"** : Used correctly only when paired values are clearly ordered; otherwise avoided

## 5. Discipline-Specific Conventions

### 5.1 Citation Style
- All papers use (Author et al., Year) in-text format consistently
- Citations are **integrated into the flow** of the sentence, not parenthetical stubs
  - Good: "Previous studies have demonstrated that X contributes to Y (Author et al., 2020)."
  - Not: "X contributes to Y. (Author et al., 2020)"
- Multiple citations grouped as "(Author1 et al., 2020; Author2 et al., 2021)"
- Superscript numbered references used in ACS journals (ES&T)

### 5.2 Abbreviation Convention
- Full term with abbreviation in parentheses **first use only**: "microalgal-bacterial granular sludge (MBGS)" or "dissolved organic matter (DOM)"
- Abbreviation used thereafter
- Abbreviation must be redefined if the term has not appeared for several paragraphs

### 5.3 Section Naming
- "1. Introduction" (no section title variants)
- "2. Materials and methods" (most common) or "2. Methods and materials"
- "3. Results and discussion" (combined) or split into "3. Results" + "4. Discussion"
- "4. Conclusions" or "5. Conclusions"
- "CRediT authorship contribution statement"
- "Declaration of Competing Interest"
- "Acknowledgements"
- "Supplementary materials"
- "Data availability" (required in newer papers)
